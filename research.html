Mental Health Research

Evidence-based insights that guide our approach to student wellness at GNLU

Research-Driven Approach

Building Wellness Programs on Scientific Foundation

Our mental health initiatives are grounded in peer-reviewed research and evidence-based practices. We continuously study the effectiveness of our programs and adapt based on the latest findings in student mental health and well-being.

Research Foundation

Key studies that inform our approach to student wellness

NAMI Study

National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) Research

NAMI's comprehensive research demonstrates that peer-led support groups and initiatives effectively reduce stigma, increase access to mental health resources, and provide a crucial sense of community for students.

Key Findings

  • Peer-led initiatives reduce mental health stigma by 40%
  • Increased access to mental health resources by 60%
  • Enhanced sense of community and belonging
  • Improved help-seeking behavior among students

Impact on GWIC

This research directly informs our peer-support programs and community-based approach to mental health awareness.

Johns Hopkins Study

Johns Hopkins University Research

Published in the Journal of College Student Development, this study found that active student engagement in campus activities leads to significantly higher levels of overall well-being and productivity.

Key Outcomes

  • Higher levels of overall well-being
  • Increased life satisfaction
  • Better social integration
  • Reduced stress levels
  • Significantly boosted productivity

Application at GWIC

These findings support our emphasis on active participation in wellness activities and community engagement.

Mental Health in Legal Education

Specialized research on mental health challenges in law schools

Law students face unique mental health challenges due to the demanding nature of legal education, competitive environment, and high-stress academic requirements. Research specific to law student mental health helps us tailor our approach.

Law Student Mental Health Crisis

43% Experience depression
37% Report anxiety disorders
25% Consider substance use problematic
